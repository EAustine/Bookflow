The bottom sheet system.

The primary modal pattern in Bookflow. Five recurring shapes, one consistent anatomy, one motion language.

Why bottom sheets

The primary modal pattern.

Bookflow uses bottom sheets instead of alert dialogs, modal overlays, or full-screen modals for ~95% of contextual actions. Three reasons: they preserve context (you can see the page underneath, dimmed), they're physically reachable on mobile (one-thumb operation), and they reinforce a calmer mental model than a dialog popping center-screen.

A short inventory of where bottom sheets appear. Recognising these as one consistent pattern with five variants — rather than seven different things — makes the system maintainable.

Anatomy

The four parts.

Every bottom sheet is built from four optional parts: handle (always), header (sometimes), body (always), footer (sometimes). Knowing which parts to include defines the variant.

Sheet title

Body content goes here. Can be a list, a form, a confirmation message — anything appropriate to the variant.

01 · Backdrop
ink-900 at 40% opacity. Tap dismisses the sheet. Fades in with the sheet's slide-up animation.
02 · Drag handle
4×32px, ink-300, centered. 8px from top edge. Always present — signals this surface can be dragged. Functional, not decorative.
03 · Header (optional)
Sheet title (Fraunces, 16px, opsz 24) + close button (28×28 circle, cream-100). Bottom border separates from body unless explicitly removed.
04 · Body
Padding 16px. Scrolls when content exceeds available space. The variant determines what goes here.
05 · Footer (optional)
Sticky at bottom. 12·16·18px padding. Top border. Holds primary CTA when present. Padding-bottom is 18px to keep CTA above iOS home indicator.
Five variants

One pattern, five shapes.

All five share the anatomy above. They differ in which parts are present, what's in the body, and whether they have a footer CTA. Mapping every contextual modal to one of these five keeps the system coherent.

Drag-to-dismiss behavior

Users can drag the sheet down to dismiss. The sheet follows the finger with rubber-band resistance past the top. Below 25% drag distance OR insufficient velocity, it snaps back.

Usage rules

Six rules for sheets.

Rules that prevent the most common ways bottom sheet systems fragment over time.

  1. 01
    Never stack sheets on top of sheets.
    If sheet B needs to open from sheet A, dismiss A first (200ms), pause briefly, then open B. Stacked sheets create visual confusion and break the "one decision at a time" principle.
  2. 02
    Confirmation sheets require explicit CTAs — never tap-to-dismiss, always stacked.
    High-stakes decisions like spending credits or deleting data must be confirmed via a tap on a real button, not by accidentally tapping a backdrop. CTAs always stack vertically (primary on top, tertiary below) — never side-by-side. Stacking gives the primary action breathing room and prevents text truncation; it also signals visual hierarchy that side-by-side erases.
  3. 03
    Sheets max out at 85% of screen height.
    Past 85%, the dimmed background context is barely visible — at that point, use a full-screen route instead. The whole point of sheets is preserving spatial context.
  4. 04
    Footers are sticky; bodies scroll.
    When body content exceeds available space, only the body scrolls. The footer with primary CTA stays visible. Never make the user scroll to find the action button.
  5. 05
    Drag handle is always present, even when there's a close button.
    The handle teaches users "this surface can be dragged." Removing it on sheets that have a close button still leaves drag gestures functional but undiscoverable. Both affordances coexist.
  6. 06
    Keyboard avoidance: sheet grows, doesn't shrink.
    When an input inside a sheet is focused and the keyboard opens, the sheet expands its height to push content above the keyboard. It does NOT shrink the sheet body to fit. This keeps the visual mental model stable.
