Six patterns

Recipes for the slots.

Each pattern is a defined combination of what fills the three slots. Naming them gives engineering and design a shared vocabulary — instead of "another row with a chevron," it's "Settings pattern."

Usage rules

Six rules.

Constraints that prevent the most common ways list-row systems fragment over time.

  1. 01
    Every row maps to exactly one of the six patterns.
    If a new context "needs" a 7th pattern, the patterns are wrong — revisit the system. Don't invent one-offs.
  2. 02
    Chevrons mean "drills into more" — never use them for actions.
    Restore purchases doesn't get a chevron. Settings → Display does. The chevron is a navigation affordance, not a generic tap indicator.
  3. 03
    Tap target is the entire row, not just the label.
    Including toggle rows — tapping anywhere on a toggle row should flip the switch. The toggle still animates, but the entire row is the hit area.
  4. 04
    Disabled rows must explain why in the sub-label.
    "Translation language" disabled with sub-label "Available with Pro" — never just dim a row without context. Mystery is friction.
  5. 05
    Destructive rows always trigger confirmation, never fire on tap.
    Sign out, Delete account, Remove book — all open a confirmation bottom sheet. The row is the trigger, not the action.
  6. 06
    Group related rows with section labels, never mix patterns within a single list.
    Toggles in their own list, settings rows in another, destructive at the bottom. Mixing patterns in one bordered list creates visual noise.
